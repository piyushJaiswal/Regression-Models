Conclusion 1: The p value associated with the F statistic is 3.7932 10-7. Since the p value is low, we can say that a relationship exists.

Also, there is a strong pattern observed in the plot of residuals vs fitted values, assuming linearity in the data. Presence of this strong pattern suggests non-linearity in the relationship.

We also observe that the most significant p value is for wt. However since our variable of concern is am, we shall start building our model with that and move on with the others.

Section 2. In this section we shall see try to understand the nature of the relationship that the response mileage per gallon holds with each of the respective quantitative predictor variables thorugh feature plots of response vs predictor

pairs(mtcars[c(1,3:7)], panel = panel.smooth, main = "MTCARS data")

Conclusion 2: The plot clearly shows a non linear (mostly quadratic) association for the predictors disp and hp. However, the predictors wt, drat and qsec can well approximated by a least squares line.

Section 3. In this section , we will do a nested model testing for seeing which of the predictors are significant.We will carry out the test based on the p-Value for the t-statistic of the individual predictors in the model that was made with all the predictors.

3.1. Our first block includes variables for the weight, disp, drat and horsepower.

pValue<-pValue[-2,]
fit1<-lm(mpg~factor(am), data=mtcars)
fit2<-update(fit1,mpg~factor(am)+wt)
fit3<-update(fit2,mpg~factor(am)+wt+qsec)
fit4<-update(fit3,mpg~factor(am)+wt+qsec+hp+I(hp^2))
fit5<-update(fit4,mpg~factor(am)+wt+qsec+hp+I(hp^2)+disp+I(disp^2))
fit6<-update(fit5,mpg~factor(am)+wt+qsec+hp+I(hp^2)+disp+I(disp^2)+drat)
fit7<-update(fit6,mpg~factor(am)+wt+qsec+hp+I(hp^2)+disp+I(disp^2)+drat+factor(gear))
result1<-anova(fit1,fit2,fit3,fit4,fit5,fit6,fit7)
